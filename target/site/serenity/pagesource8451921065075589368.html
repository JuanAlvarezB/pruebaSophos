              <div class="field-name-body table-of-content-body"> 

               <p>Behavior-driven development (BDD) practices can help your teams <a href="https://content.microfocus.com/world-quality-report-tb/quality-development-qa?utm_source=techbeacon&utm_medium=website&utm_campaign=7014J000000dVA4QAM" target="_blank">build better software </a>by making them carefully specify the products behaviors using plain-language examples. And whether you're seeking better collaboration through "<a href="http://angiejones.tech/writing-good-gherkin-enables-good-test-automation/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow">three amigos</a>" meetings or wanting to automate better using a framework such as Cucumber, one language rests at the center of <a href="https://automationpanda.com/BDD/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow">the BDD movement</a>: Gherkin.</p>

               <p>On the surface, Gherkin looks easyjust write some steps to describe the desired behavior. However, Ive seen several teams struggle with writing Gherkin well. Beginners often get writers block, or they write scenarios that <a href="https://content.microfocus.com/software-test-automation-tb/top-7-test-automation-mistakes?lx=wYdl7c&utm_source=techbeacon&utm_medium=techbeacon&utm_campaign=7014J000000dVA4QAM" target="_blank">cant easily be automated</a>. Sometimes, ambiguity leads people to interpret steps in different ways. Other times, people provide too much detail in their steps, which makes scenarios hard to understand. BDD even gets a bad rap due to frustrations with Gherkin.</p>

               <p>Writing good Gherkin may be challenging, but it certainly isnt impossible. Here are four rules that will help you to write readable, automatable, scalable Gherkin.</p>

               <h3>1. Gherkin's Golden Rule</h3>

               <p>Gherkins Golden Rule is simple: Treat other readers as you would want to be treated. More specifically, write feature files so that everyone can intuitively understand them. Good Gherkin should improve team collaboration by clarifying behaviors you want to develop. When Gherkin is tough for others to read, teams cant develop good behaviors.</p>

               <p>Lets say your team is developing a website for a shoe store. Consider the following scenario: </p>

               <p><code><span class="monospace">Scenario: Shoes<br>  Given I want shoes<br>  When I buy shoes<br>  Then I get them</span></code></p>

               <p>This scenario is <i>ultra-declarative</i> because it doesnt provide enough information about the desired behavior. Its too vague. What shoes? How are they purchased? How will you verify the purchase? Its also not automatable because the steps are not procedural. The fact that the narrator wants shoes is part of the user story, not the specification. The scenario also lacks accountability because it omits any clear conditions for success. Typically, product owners are more likely to write ultra-declarative scenarios such as this because they arent familiar with automation.</p>

               <p>Now, consider the opposite extreme: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario: Purchase shoes through the app<br>  Given my username is "jessica8494"<br>  And my password is "PleaseDontPutSecretsInGherkin"<br>  When I navigate the browser to "www.shoestore.com"<br>  And I enter my username into the "Username" field<br>  And I enter my password into the "Password" field<br>  And I click the login button<br>  And I wait "10" seconds<br>  Then the Shoe Store home page is displayed<br>  And the title banner contains the text "Shoe Store"<br>  And the shopping cart is empty<br>  When I click the search bar<br>  And I type "red pumps" into the search bar<br>  And I click the search button<br>  And I wait "30" seconds<br>  Then the search results page is displayed<br>  And the search results page shows "10" results per page<br>  And ...</code></p>

               <p>This scenario is ultra-imperative because it provides too much detail. All the steps name manual, low-level interactions such as clicking buttons and waiting for seconds to pass. They overwhelm the reader with a wall of text that obfuscates rather than clarifies the value of this scenario. You can automate each step, but collaboration will be tough.</p>

               <p>If you were on this team, would you want to be assigned either of these scenarios? Certainly not, since neither helps to build the desired behavior. Heres how to write these: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario: Add shoes to the shopping cart<br>  Given the shoe store home page is displayed<br>  When the shopper searches for "red pumps"<br>  And the shopper adds the first result to the cart<br>  Then the cart has one pair of "red pumps"</code></p>

               <p>This scenario has declarative, descriptive steps. The steps strictly follow a <em>given-when-then </em>order that clearly shows the setup, the interaction, and the verification of the desired behavior. The scenario uses red pumps? as a concrete example to help the reader better understand the scenario. The scenario is concise, yet unambiguous. Its a much better scenario than the others.</p>

               <p>If you arent sure if your scenarios are too declarative or too imperative, refer to Gherkins Golden Rule. What kind of scenario would you want to read?</p>

               <h3>2. The cardinal rule of BDD</h3>

               <p>The cardinal rule of BDD is a one-to-one rule: One scenario should cover exactly one single, independent behavior. Focusing on one behavior at a time has several benefits:</p>

               <ul>

                <li><strong>Collaboration:</strong> More focus and less confusion</li>

                <li><strong>Automation: </strong>Each test failure points to a unique problem</li>

                <li><strong>Efficiency:</strong> Less complex work makes for faster cycle times</li>

                <li><strong>Traceability:</strong> 1 behavior  1 example  1 scenario  1 test  1 result</li>

                <li><strong>Accountability: </strong>Teams cannot hide or avoid behaviors</li>

               </ul>

               <p>Thankfully, its easy to spot a scenario that covers more than one behavior. Consider this scenario: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario: Simple product search<br>  Given the shoe store home page is displayed<br>  When the search phrase "red pumps" is entered<br>  Then results for "red pumps" are shown<br>  When the user searches for images from the results page<br>  Then image results for "red pumps" are shown</code></p>

               <p>This scenario covers two separate behaviors: searching for shoes, and searching for images of shoes. Each <em>when-then</em> pair denotes a unique behavior. Whenever a scenario has more than one <em>when-then</em> pair, it inherently covers more than one behavior. Thankfully, splitting scenarios is easy: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario: Simple Web search<br>  Given the shoe store home page is displayed<br>  When the search phrase "red pumps" is entered<br>  Then results for "red pumps" are shown<br><br>Scenario: Simple Web image search<br>  Given shoe store search results for "red pumps" are displayed<br>  When the user searches for images from the results page<br>  Then image results for "red pumps" are shown</code></p>

               <p>You can capture the stopping point for the first scenario as a new <em>given</em> step for the second scenario. In this way, the second scenario focuses only on its unique behavior. You can also use automation to try to optimize this <em>given</em> step by taking shortcuts such as a direct link.</p>

               <h3>3. The unique example rule</h3>

               <p>Sometimes, less is more, so dont include unnecessary examples. Focus instead on unique equivalence classes. Testing all the things? might sound tempting, but that wastes precious time. Instead, test the most important things, and avoid testing duplicate things.</p>

               <p>Consider the following scenario: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario Outline: Simple product search<br>  Given the shoe store home page is displayed<br>  When the search phrase "<phrase>" is entered<br>  Then results for "<phrase>" are shown<br> <br>  Examples: Shoes<br>    | phrase        |<br>    | red pumps     |<br>    | sneakers      |<br>    | sandals       |<br>    | flip flops    |<br>    | flats         |<br>    | slippers      |<br>    | running shoes |</code></p>

               <p>Scenario outlines run the scenario, one for each row in the examples table, so this scenario outline would run seven times. If a typical web UI test takes one minute, then each run will add a lot of time to the whole test suite. All of these examples just cover different types of shoes. Arguably, shoes? could be an equivalence class, so testing different types of shoes may not be worthwhile. Always carefully consider the examples chosen.</p>

               <h3>4. The good grammar rule</h3>

               <p>Language matters, so write as though your high school English teacher will be grading your Gherkin. Behavior scenarios are meant to be readable and expressive. Steps are meant to be reusable. Poor grammar, misspellings, and inconsistent phrasing can ruin the benefits of behavior specification. Scenarios can become confusing, and team members could use steps out of context.</p>

               <p>One of the biggest language problems with Gherkin is an inconsistent point of view. You can write steps using first-person perspective (I, me, my?) or third-person perspective (the user ?). While I <a href="https://automationpanda.com/2017/01/18/should-gherkin-steps-use-first-person-or-third-person/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow">strongly favor third-person perspective</a> for Gherkin steps, it is more important for a solution to use one or the other <i>exclusively</i>. Look at what happens when steps mix the point of view: </p>

               <p><code>Scenario: Simple product search<br>  Given I am at the shoe store page<br>  When the user searches for "red pumps"<br>  Then I see web page links for "red pumps"</code></p>

               <p>This scenario is understandable, but looks sloppy. The meaning could also be ambiguous: Am I? the user,? or is there a second user? Use one point of view for consistency.</p>

               <p>Poor step phrases are another common problem. Your steps should use subject-predicate phrases, just as you would when writing regular sentences. Imagine seeing the following steps: </p>

               <p><code>  And image links for "sneakers"<br>  And video links for "sneakers"</code></p>

               <p>Out of context, these steps are ambiguous. Do they click the links? Do they verify the existence of the links? They simply specify a subject, not a predicate. This type of ambiguity can cause problems when a team tries to write and automate new scenarios. Clarify them like this: </p>

               <p><code>  And the results page shows image links for "sneakers"<br>  And the results page shows video links for "sneakers"</code></p>

               <p>A third problem I frequently see involves tense: past, present, and future. Each type of step should follow consistent rules within a solution. I recommend the following:</p>

               <ul>

                <li><em>Given</em> steps should use past or present-perfect tense, because they represent an initial state that must already be established.</li>

                <li><em>When</em> steps should use present tense, because they represent actions actively performed as part of the behavior.</li>

                <li><em>Then</em> steps should use present or future tense, because they represent what should happen after the behavior actions.</li>

               </ul>

               <h3>Practice makes perfect</h3>

               <p>The rules Ive shared above should be your guiding principles for good Gherkin. I could give you a bunch of rules about formatting, line length, or word choice, but those are secondary details to good principles. So give it a try, and have fun writing good Gherkin.</p>

               <p><i>Want to learn more about those other rules about Gherkin and BDD? Read my <a href="https://automationpanda.com/bdd/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow">in-depth tutorial</a>. </i></p>
